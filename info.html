Your Health Matters

Over 4,000 men in Aotearoa, New Zealand, receive a prostate cancer diagnosis each year, and 650 of them pass away as a result of the disease. Prostate cancer is the third leading cause of cancer-related deaths among males in New Zealand and the most frequent type of cancer overall.

The Prostate & Health

The prostate is a gland below the bladder and in front of the rectum in male. It consists of connective tissues and glandular tissues. It adds fluid to semen, and its muscles help push semen through your urethra. Conditions that affect your prostate include cancer, prostatitis and benign prostatic hyperplasia.

The prostate gland is surrounded by a capsule of connective tissue containing many smooth muscle fibers and elastic connective tissue, which is why it feels very elastic to the touch when it is examined. There are also many smooth muscle cells inside of the prostate. During ejaculation these muscle cells contract and forcefully press the fluid that has been stored in the prostate out into the urethra. This causes the fluid and the sperm cells, together with fluid from other glands, to combine to form semen, which is then released.

Prostate Cancer

In New Zealand, prostate cancer is the most prevalent disease diagnosed in men, although in its early stages, it can often go unnoticed. Here, it ranks as the third leading cause of cancer-related deaths in men. This type of cancer has unknown etiology. We do know that early identification can be crucial to effective treatment and that genetics may be involved. However, there are several obstacles to cancer treatment, such as cost and accessibility, which explains why our prostate cancer rates are so high. If you cannot afford to go private, the waiting lists for surgery and certain diagnostic procedures are quite long, and the cost of cancer medications might be prohibitive in New Zealand.

Understanding Prostate Cancer

Signs & Symptoms

Frequent urination, difficulty starting, pain or burning, and blood in urine or semen may be signs to watch.

Prevention Strategies

Healthy diet, regular exercise, reducing red meat, and early screenings help lower your risk.
